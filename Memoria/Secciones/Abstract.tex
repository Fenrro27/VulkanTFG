\chapter*{Abstract}

This Final Degree Project describes the design and implementation of a high-performance rendering engine based on the Vulkan API, oriented towards the generation and visualization of complex particle systems in real time. The core of the project lies in the creation of a system capable of simulating visual phenomena such as fire, smoke, and water through the use of Compute Shaders. This architecture enables the transfer of the entire computational load of the particle lifecycle (position, color, and size updates) entirely to the GPU, eliminating traditional bottlenecks between the CPU and video memory.

On a technical level, the engine explicitly manages hardware resources through a modular structure that includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Computation Pipeline: Implements procedural logic and noise to provide organic movement to smoke (sinuous vortices) and fire (flickering effect and dynamic ascent).
    \item Advanced Synchronization: Uses memory barriers (VkBufferMemoryBarrier) to guarantee data integrity between the physical computation stages and the final graphic rendering.
    \item Particle Rendering: Employs Point Sprites and Alpha Blending techniques to achieve the transparency and visual density necessary in volumetric effects.
    \item Complete Graphical Environment: The system is integrated into a 3D scene that includes ADS lighting (Ambient, Diffuse, Specular), texture mapping, 3D models, and a dynamic background via Skybox with cubemaps.
\end{itemize}

The results demonstrate Vulkan's efficiency in handling thousands of concurrent particles, achieving smooth and highly parameterizable visual representation, laying the foundation for applications in video games and interactive visual simulations.

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Vulkan, Compute Shaders, Particle Systems, Real-Time, GPU, Computer Graphics.
